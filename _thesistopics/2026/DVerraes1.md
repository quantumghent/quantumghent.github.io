#### Context

In 2023, a major breakthrough occurred with the experimental observation of high-temperature (Tc ≈ 80K) superconductivity in bulk samples of La3Ni2O7 (n = 2 in Fig. 1) at hydrostatic pressures above 14 GPa [1]. The pronounced structural and electronic differences compared to other unconventional superconductors have thereby inspired a plethora of theoretical studies aimed at uncovering the pairing mechanism in this layered nickelate. However, in these works, the simplified two-dimensional models proposed to study pairing order are not derived from first principles. This limitation restricts the electronic and structural insights they can provide for materials design toward stabilising superconductivity in nickelates under ambient pressure or at higher temperatures.

In recent work [2], we derived effective model Hamiltonians for the few relevant electrons in pressurised La₃Ni₂O₇ using the downfolding methodology [3]. The obtained low-energy models revealed a significant increase in on-site repulsion strength for the electronic bands crossing the Fermi energy, specifically in the pressure range where superconductivity is observed. This increase is attributed to a pressure-driven interplay between orbital localisation and competing screening channels arising from structural variations. These findings pave the way towards the deliberate design of superconductivity in the  Ruddlesden-Popper nickelate series An+1NinO3n+1 (Fig. 1), in which the A-site cation, layer index n and (anisotropic) pressure-dependence provide rich flexibility. This is the objective of this ambitious and highly relevant dissertation.

#### Goal

The goal of this Master’s dissertation is the structural design of An+1NinO3n+1 by studying the influence of chemical substitution, different stoichiometries (n) and polymorphism, as well as the influence of epitaxial compression on the low-energy physics. First, density functional theory (DFT) and molecular dynamics simulations will be performed to understand the crystal structure of these quasi-2D compounds. Using the resulting in situ crystal structures as a starting point, we will analyse the strongly correlated subspace of the electronic structure, as it plays a dominant role in the superconducting state. Over the past four years, a downfolding renormalisation framework has been actively developed within our group, providing the student with established methods to construct effective Hamiltonians for the few strongly correlated electrons by computing the screening effects of the other “high-energy” electrons. The resulting model parameters will gain first-principles insights into the correlations of these compounds and will allow to reverse engineer towards a stable superconducting phase at ambient pressure or higher temperatures.

To gain familiarity with DFT and downfolding techniques, the student will first apply them to monolayer La2NiO4, as well as La3Ni2O7 in its metastable polymorph featuring monolayer-trilayer structures, and trilayer La4Ni3O10, the latter two of which have shown signs of unconventional superconductivity under pressure. This will enable an ab initio comparison of the superconducting phase with our recent results on bilayer La3Ni2O7. Once the downfolding methodology for these quasi-2D models is well established, it will be extended to more complex nickelates through chemical substitution at the A-site and the application of anisotropic pressure, which can be achieved experimentally via epitaxial compression.
